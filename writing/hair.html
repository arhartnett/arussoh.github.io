Hair

Andrew Hartnett

The hair fell around her feet like snow. Hair is itchy, and knowing it’s someone else’s only makes it worse. She worked methodically, the clippers vibrating in her hand. Her arms grew tired and she paused a minute to stretch her shoulders and keep blood flowing. The whole time, the kid in her chair stared down at his lap, unresponsive.

“Do you want me to take off the sideburns?” she asked when she was done buzzing his head with a number 4 on top, number 2 on the sides. He said nothing. She looked over to his mother, but she was buried in a magazine. So the sideburns came off.

When the boy was done, he scampered off to his mother and the hairdresser rang up the cut. It was fourteen dollars, and the mother slid a ten and a five across the desk.

“Keep the change,” the mother said, and the hairdresser forced a smile.

“If you’re interested, there’s a survey on the bottom of your receipt. Bring it in for ten percent off your next cut. My name is Meredith, and the name of our location is Unisex Hair Palace, Meyers Plaza.” In the two months since the survey program had started, only one had been brought back in, by an old woman who hadn’t even wanted the ten percent off, just to tell to tell her stylist Mindy what a lovely young lady she was. When the family left, Meredith sat in her chair, watching her two coworkers for the day fiddle with their combs and scissors. It wasn’t normally a chatty environment. Each one of them worked off commission and tips, and although the workplace code of conduct demanded they share clients evenly, there was always competition over who got which customers.

 

As the last few minutes before seven ticked away, Meredith counted the tips she’d wadded up in her pocket: forty-three dollars. She took two paper clips from the front desk stowed the money in her bag in two neatly folded stacks. She pulled her coat over her shoulders and held it closed, the zipper didn’t work anymore, and walked out to her car.

The worn seat of her car might as well have been a block of ice when she fell into it, tossing her bag onto the passenger seat. She sat there for a full minute in the empty parking lot, watching her breath spiral in a fog to the windshield where the frost made crystalline rainbows from the halogen lights outside. It was quiet, most people were home long before seven this time of winter. The only activity came from the loading dock behind the grocery store, where a semitrailer fresh in from the supply center in Butte was dropping off the next day’s wares.

The car started on the third try, which was typical given the temperatures, and she eased out of the lot and onto a deserted Main Street. Her commute was less than five minutes, not nearly enough time for the heat to do much good, so she spent the drive clutching her broken coat with her left hand, trying to keep most of the cold air out.

She could see the blue light of the television reflect against the living room when she pulled into her driveway. Jack didn’t look up from the screen when she walked in the door, too engrossed in the virtual war his thumbs were commanding on the old PlayStation. It wasn’t until the gust of cold air jolted him from his activity that he paused the game.

“Meri Berry!” he said, using a name that Meredith hated. “How was work, cute cheeks?”

Jack lay on the couch, shirtless, the zipper of his jeans halfway undone. There were three empty cans of beer on the floor next to the couch, and a few Pop Tart crumbs were tangled in his chest hair.

“It was fine,” said Meredith, unloading her bag onto the kitchen table.

“You make some good money?” he asked. He reached out to grab her waist, but she sidestepped his reach.

“Yep,” she said. “Thirty-three dollars.”

Jack whistled. “You pull that in a four-hour shift? I’m starting to worry you’re spending time on more than one head, if you know what I’m saying.”

Again he reached for her, but she rolled her eyes and walked into the kitchen.

“I’m not cooking for you tonight,” she called back to him. “I’m too tired.”

“I know, I already ate,” he said.

“Pop Tarts?”

“Four of them,” he said proudly.

“You know you can also cook for yourself,” she said.

“I did. Used the toaster and everything.”

Meredith filled a glass of water and went to the bedroom.

“Don’t come in, I’m getting changed,” she yelled.

“What’s the sense of being married if I don’t even get to watch you strip for me?” he said, but the sounds of his video game had already resumed. She locked the door anyway.

Inside her closet, she moved aside her laundry basket and reached into the far right corner until she found what she was looking for: a box of tampons. Inside, in a few dozen similar wads, was more than five hundred dollars, mostly in singles. She added the latest ten to the pile, and put the money back in its hiding place. Two bus tickets to Boise would be about $120, and she’d found an apartment there with a $300 deposit and $250 per month rent. She was close.

 

Meredith had a double shift that Friday, and it was good luck because the local high school was having some kind of winter dance the next day. Her favorite were the boys, who sheepishly asked her to make them look presentable for their dates, then over tipped because they were awkward and didn’t know what they were doing. The girls, on the other hand, generally fussed too much, but at least their hair was softer, and less itchy. She managed to convince a few young men to buy conditioner, only on the assurance that it was meant for men, which added a few dollars to her commission for the day, and hopefully made the job of whoever cut their hair next a little easier.

She got half an hour for lunch, and spent every second of it somewhere other than the salon. As soon as the time machine had finished beeping her into the unpaid half-hour, she was out the door into the biting cold. She walked down the cracked sidewalk, clutching the thermos of soup she’d microwaved, while still on the clock, to her chest for warmth. The hair salon had its own break room, but it smelled like hair and hair product and Meredith never felt like she was actually taking a break while there. The grocery store had its own breakroom, where she was headed now.

The door to the breakroom had a window, and through it she spied Caleb, in his coveralls. Caleb was smaller than most of the guys who worked at the loading dock, but from Meredith’s experience he was good with his hands. Really, though, they probably kept him around for his personality, he had that intangible ability to make a room feel happier. Not to mention, his foul shooting helped their pickup basketball team against the tire place at the other end of the shopping plaza.

Meredith rapped on the glass, and Caleb smiled when he saw her through the window. He excused himself and walked out to meet her.

“I went out and started it up about ten minutes ago,” he said. “Hopefully the heat will be going by now.”

Indeed, when they climbed into the black pickup truck, the air inside was a cozy contrast to the frosty outside. It wasn’t Caleb’s car, it belonged to the supermarket and Caleb was only supposed to use it for work related jobs, but no one was coming to check on them at this point in the day. Caleb spun the radio dial to an oldies station as Meredith carefully opened her soup in the passenger seat, then he leaned over and gently kissed her. Jack was a more technically talented kisser than Caleb, but Jack never bothered to shave and his whiskers ruined the experience. Meredith had heard all kinds of things about Caleb. It was probably true that he had screwed half the girls who worked in her salon, but she didn’t mind that especially.

“I got a surprise for you,” said Caleb as Meredith hungrily spooned soup into her mouth.

“What?” she said, wiping her mouth.

“I guessed the right score for the Super Bowl this year. Even got right the time of the first of touchdown.”

“Congrats,” Meredith said, laughing. Her laughter faltered when Caleb pulled a paper envelope an inside pocket.

“No, see, I put money on it too.” Caleb looked around to make sure no one was watching before he opened the envelope. Inside was a thick stack of bills, most of them twenties.

“Oh my- how much?” Meredith said, her eyes wide.

“One thousand four hundred thirty-one,” said Caleb. “Won the whole damn pot.”

Meredith put her soup in the truck’s cup holder and threw her arms around his neck.

“This means-”

“I know,” he said, beaming.

“Tomorrow?” she asked, breathless.

“If you want. I checked, bus leaves at 6am.”

 

Meredith spent the last four hours of her shift with her heart thudding in her ears. She left one girl in tears when her perm job turned out all wrong. The girl screamed at her as she left without paying.

“You worthless bitch,” she yelled. “You ruined my life.”

Meredith left the salon that night in good spirits, knowing it was probably the last time she’d ever be in that parking lot. When she got home, she was surprised to not see the light of the television, and even more surprised to hear the sound of the stove top working when she stepped inside.

“What are you doing in there?” she asked Jack, who was staring at a pot of boiling water with all of his attention.

“I’m making you dinner,” he said, pointing to the table where he’d even set up two candles. “Spaghetti and meatballs.”

It was the first time Jack had ever cooked dinner, and it admittedly showed. The fact that he’d tried, though, made her not mind the soggy pasta or the burned meatballs or the sauce that was actually pizza sauce. That night, they had sex, and Meredith enjoyed it for the first time in three years.

Her alarm went off at five in the morning. The lack of sleep made her eye sockets feel massively heavy, but she quietly rolled out of bed anyway.

“Where you going, cute cheeks?” Jack groggily asked her.

“I have to work early,” she whispered back. Jack nodded and went back to sleep.

Meredith watched him for a moment, his naked body contorted in the bed like a wasted man. His workman’s comp ran out next month, and he’d have to go back to work at the mill. That might help with the gut he’d developed in his four months off the job, but even at his best, he was far from physically attractive.

She quickly threw her favorite clothes into a suitcase, taking care not to make their absence too obvious, lest Jack realize what she’d done and chase after her before they’d reached the state line. She counted up her saved money, six hundred and seven dollars all told, and stuffed it into her purse, then headed outside into the bitter cold. The bus stop was only a fifteen-minute walk, and the car was technically Jack’s and she didn’t want to get charged with stealing it, so she bowed her head to the wind and marched down the street.

The fluorescent lights of the bus station were a welcoming sight. Caleb was already there, having gotten a ride with some buddies from work before telling them he was quitting. He offered her coffee and showed her the tickets he’d just bought.

“Looks like this is happening,” he said, excited.

Meredith had expected to feel more excitement at this moment, more spontaneity. But she’d been planning and dreaming of it for so long that it just felt normal. Here she was, running away from her husband with the man she’d had an affair with, and all she could think of was how tired she felt. Maybe that’s how it’s supposed to be, she thought. Maybe that meant it she was confident it was the right thing to do.

In three months, after both she and Caleb had started new jobs, him as a short order cook in a diner and her as, once again, a stylist, though at a nicer salon, Meredith would find out she was pregnant. Six months after that, the baby would come out with Jack’s black hair instead of Caleb’s blonde. She would call back to her own house, and get a disconnected signal. She’d phone around town and eventually find out Jack had killed himself two weeks after she’d left. She’d also find out from Mrs. Jenkins, who loved to talk, that the girl whose hair Meredith had ruined had broken up with her boyfriend at the dance, then had sex with the gym teacher behind the bleachers. The gym teacher was in jail now, Mrs. Jenkins would say, and the girl had dropped out of school.

All these things would happen, but they weren’t a part of Meredith’s decision as she and Caleb found seats next to each other before the ten-hour bus ride.

“So how much did you end up with?” asked Caleb.

“What?” said Meredith.

“Money. How much did you manage to save up?”

“Oh,” said Meredith. “Four hundred and seven dollars.”
