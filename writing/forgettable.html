Forgettable

Andrew Hartnett

The gray-suited men came to town on an otherwise colorful day. It was early fall, when the air tasted dusty and comfortable. Their black Escalade slipped quietly down Main Street, passing the grocer, both auto parts stores, and the tiny library, before crunching to a stop on the shoulder about two minutes outside town. The men stepped out, chatting cordially, and walked a short way down the road. They looked out over the red and gold capped trees that lined the field, but stayed on the asphalt. Their shiny black shoes were the wrong kind of leather for mud.

Across the road, Arthur Meyer watched from the ripped seat of his tractor, an oil stained hat shading his wrinkled face and a pinch of tobacco in his cheek. He had only driven out that way to watch what the men were doing. He was too old to use the tractor for any of the farm work it was meant for. As Arthur watched, a red truck bounced down the road and parked just behind the black car. Three men in jeans hopped out and set at once to work, unloading pieces of wood from the truck and hammering them together until an enormous billboard stood squarely in the middle of the field, facing the road.

“Coming Soon!” it shouted. “The Latest P&M Pharmacy!”

These sentences stood above and below a large picture of the plainest building Arthur had ever seen. He would describe the building to his son back at the house as square, and not much else.

“They’re putting that on Tim Shuart’s land?” Robert, his son, asked as he set two plates out for dinner.

“Ain’t his anymore,” said Arthur, lowering himself painfully into his chair at the head of the table. “When he died last winter it went to his kids.”

“Why didn’t they keep working it?” asked Robert.

“Well. His oldest son’s down in Florida, says he got a good job with the parks service. The other one moved out to California or San Francisco or something. He never liked farming anyhow. Didn’t like working.”

“Never liked that kid back in high school.”

“You wouldn’t. You were always a worker Bobby. You like the work.”

“I like the farm,” said Robert.

“Sure do,” said Arthur, shaking his head.

 

The air was still sharp with frost when Robert pulled a pair of dirty work boots over his wool socks, grabbed the leash from its peg by the door, and took Molly, the farm’s German shepherd, for a walk. Molly had retired from her days as the barn’s watch dog when Arthur, Robert’s dad, had sold the last of his cows, counting on fields of corn to pay for his retirement, or the closest thing to retirement a small-time farmer could hope for. Even though Molly lived outside, and was allowed to run anywhere on the farm’s hundred acres, she still liked being taken on walks. Her favorite route, or rather Robert’s favorite route that Molly didn’t seem to mind, took them down and across the road and into the old rural cemetery.

The cemetery was perched atop, and on two sides of, a small hill. It had once, presumably, been part of the farm that had belonged to Tim Shuart. The area had been too steep for the manual tools of the day, Robert surmised, and long gone generations of farmers had started burying their family there. A wall had gone up, a few trees had been planted, and they had a cemetery. No one was being buried there anymore; a much larger, less rustic cemetery had been incorporated in 1925 on the other side of town. Everyone buried in the old plot was long gone, their headstones weathered, forgotten.

Robert spent most of the walk thinking and kicking at bits of gravel, and Molly spent it chasing the scent of a squirrel. Neither noticed the black Escalade snaking its way along the road to their driveway until they were walking back, and it was parked in the driveway.

Molly started barking as they approached the house. She smelled the car - new, different, unwanted, before Robert saw it. When they reached the lawn, Robert unhooked Molly’s leash so she could run off and play in the field, but Molly stayed, leaning forward slightly, staring intently at the car.

Robert pushed the porch door closed behind him and saw his dad sitting with two of the men in gray suits at the kitchen table. The men had left their shoes on, Robert noted unhappily. Laid out on the table was a pile of forms and contracts and laminated maps.

“This here’s my son, Robert,” Arthur told the men, smiling. The man closest to Robert stood, smoothly buttoning his coat as he reached his hand out.

“Jeff Flake,” he said. Robert ignored his hand.

“What’s going on, dad?” he asked, walking to the table and studying the maps.

“These are the fellows from the new pharmacy they’re putting in on Tim’s old field,” said Arthur.

“As we were just telling your father,” said Jeff Flake, ignoring Robert’s rebuff and sitting back down. “The new P&M Pharmacy is going to be a major economic boost to the town. We’re expecting more than 200 new jobs — stocking shelves, running registers, moving boxes in the warehouse that’s also going in on this site.” He nodded to Robert’s broad shoulders. “Jobs that are perfect for a guy like you.”

“Thanks, but I’m set for work. Running a farm means you don’t get laid off every time the stock market shits the bed.”

“It’s true, regions like this one do tend to be fairly recession proof,” said the man in a suit who wasn’t Jeff Flake. “But unfortunately, they’re also boom proof. Unemployment around here usually stays at around 20 percent. When your town only has five thousand people, 200 new jobs can make a big difference.”

“I could see you stacking boxes for a living,” said Arthur to his son. “Driving around in one of those forklifts all day. You’re a hard worker, you could work your way up.” The men nodded.

“What are you talking to us for?” asked Robert.

Jeff Flake adjusted his tie, looking at the table.

“Well, in order to have progress, there have to be changes. With a warehouse this big, and all the infrastructure it requires, and the need to accommodate heavy truck traffic…”

“They’re buying the farm from me,” said Arthur, enthusiastic.

“Yes. Our plans involve moving the roadway about 200 yards east and combining your parcel with the one across the street.”

“They’re offering twice how much I paid for it, too,” Arthur continued. “Cash. None of them realtor fees, either.”

“Dad I didn’t know you were looking to sell the farm,” said Robert, picking up one of the maps from the table.

“I didn’t look! They just came in and asked!”

But Robert was distracted by something in the map.

“Hold a second. If the roads gonna be all the way over here, how’s anyone supposed to get to the old cemetery? It looks like your warehouse is in the way.”

The men glanced at each other, and Not-Jeff-Flake spoke.

“Our records show that no one has been buried in that plot for nearly a century. It’s hardly ever visited.”

“I visit. I walk my dog up that way every morning.”

 “If you’re concerned, none of the graves will be disturbed. It’s just the access road we’re moving.”

“You’ll have to give us time to think about the offer,” said Robert, folding the map back into his pocket.

“Actually, we’d really appreciate a decision today,” said Not-Jeff-Flake. “The sooner we break ground the better.”

“Bobby, I’m too old for this shit. Literally, this shit,” he cackled. “This much cash, you’d be set for a few years at least and I’d be able to move somewhere easier in town, wouldn’t have to worry about this place anymore.”

“That’s what you got me for!” said Robert.

“Bobby, listen. I know you love this place, so I don’t want to do this without talking to you first. But this kind of offer doesn’t just happen. People don’t buy farms anymore, Bobby. This is my only way out. And my name’s the only one on the deed.”

Robert had gotten the sense that his dad was sick of the farm, but was still surprised he was so eager to leave so quickly. But it didn’t matter, his dad was right and Robert knew it. Robert nodded, Arthur signed his name, and the men left, the tires of their car squealing as the revved back up the road.

 

The first frost was due in just a few weeks, so the pharmacy wouldn’t break ground until spring. They could stay in the house until then, that was in the contract as something called an enticement clause.

He was with Molly, again, staring at one of the larger headstones. GEORGE KOBL and his wife IDA. That’s how it was with most of the women in the cemetery, always second, an accessory to the real thing. George, at least, would be remembered — one of the roads in town was named Kobl Street.

After the walk, Robert let Molly loose in the yard, and took the truck into town. It had started to rain, in the sad way autumn rain turns crisp golden leaves into brown mush. Robert parked at the library and hurried inside.

The main floor of the library was small, holding barely a thousand books, but the basement was packed with two centuries of local records. Census reports, newspaper archives, even advertisements for the county fair. Using George’s date of death as a starting point, Robert worked through the records, carefully flipping through copies of the local paper that were more than a century old. The paper was too small, and the library too underfunded, to warrant microfiche or digital archive, but each issue was stored in a plastic sheet and kept in a binder, which helped.

He wanted a story on the naming of Kobl Street, so that someone would remember something about George. There were less than a dozen roads in the town, and this was a paper that regularly ran front page features on a second grade class trip or the start of hunting season, so Robert was sure he would fine one. It took most of the afternoon, but finally, in June of 1905, a headline declared “ROAD NAMED FOR LOCAL HERO.” To Robert’s surprise, the hero was Ida Kobl, not her husband. Ida had, according to the article, been the first woman in the town’s history to drive an automobile, right down Main Street, and the town had unanimously agreed on the spot that she deserved to have her name on something.

At the top of the article there was a small, oval shaped photograph of Ida herself, standing beside George, the car, and the Kobl street sign. It was curious to put a face to a name.

He took out a notebook and starting copying details from the article. George had been an accountant, and had bought the car with money inherited from his parents. Ida was a school teacher, but had always been a thrill seeker and was exhilarated by the car’s speed. They’d lived in a house fifteen minutes up the road from Robert’s.

“You related?” said a voice behind Robert.

He jumped and turned around to see a young woman with blue eyes and her black hair pulled from her wire-frame glasses watching over his shoulder.

“No, no they’re not. Just curious about the name, like the road.”
              “So you spent all that time looking for an article just because you were curious about the name?” She leaned over the binder and scanned the article.

“I guess so. Have you been watching the whole time?”

She laughed.
              “No, but I saw you come in. I work at the front desk.” She turned to go back upstairs. “The library’s closing, but I can wait until you finish reading.”

“Oh that’s alright,” said Robert, quickly stuffing his notebook into a pocket and placing the binder back on its shelf. “I was just leaving.”

“I’m sure you were,” she said with a smile.

 

That night, Robert carefully wrote his notes from the library on a larger, nicer piece of paper, straining to make his handwriting as neat as possible. The next morning, still in the dark, he woke Molly and set off on their walk, earlier than expected. Molly was a bit bleary eyed, but followed willingly, always excited to be on a leash.

At the Kobl’s headstone, Robert looked down at their names again and smiled, thinking of the sepia photograph in the newspaper. There were well over a hundred other headstones, just like the Kobl’s. Not all of them had been famous enough to get roads named after them. But they had all lived in the town and, presumably, had done something at some point in their lives.

Robert jotted down a few names of nearby graves, and rushed off to the library. Chronologically seemed the best way to find all the names he’d written down, starting from the oldest name’s birth and working toward the youngest’s death. He pulled the handle of the painted wooden door, but it was locked. The lights were off. The parking lot was empty. It was Sunday. Nearly everything in the town was closed on Sunday. Arthur had been a traditional farmer, but wasn’t religious, so Robert was raised with no understanding of why Sunday was any different from an ordinary day.

He turned back to his truck, rubbing the back of his head, and thinking how foolish he must look to the ladies he knew were watching him from the senior center across the street.

“You back for more,” called a familiar voice from the sidewalk. The librarian walked up to him.

“Oh, I was just- I forgot about Sunday,” he said.

“I’ll let you in, if you’re curious about more street signs.”

“I don’t want to be any trouble.”

“Oh, it’s my day off. It’s no trouble.”

“Then why are you here?”

“Just walking back from church,” she said. “I remembered Sunday.”

“Oh, I never went to church as a kid.”

“Me neither, but the library runs a Sunday school program at the Methodist church, and I volunteer there once a month.”

She unlocked the door.

“Thanks for that. My name’s Robert Meyer, by the way.”

“I’m Molly. Molly Pemberton.”

“I’ve got a dog named Molly.”

“It’s a lovely name.”

“You’re not from here, are you?” asked Robert.

“I’ve lived here for the last five years.”

Robert blinked.

“But no, I grew up near Boston,” she continued. “I only moved up here because I couldn’t stand the city.”

“I’m not a fan of the city either.”

“Have you ever been?”

“Been to Buffalo.”

“That’s hardly a city.”

“Well they got enough people for a city.”

She laughed as they stepped down the stairs.

“Let’s get to work,” she said. “Who are you looking for this time?”

Robert showed her the list of names. “I live across the road from the old cemetery. I guess I just got to thinking about who all these people were, you know, before they died.”

Molly sat cross legged on the ground by the census records and pulled one of the heavy leather volumes onto her lap.

“So what kind of stuff do you want?”

“Just facts or stories, things to remember them by. I take all the good stuff and write it on nice paper.”

“And then what?”

Robert shrugged.

“I have an empty book you can start putting them in,” she said. “You can make a whole collection.”

Robert nodded and joined her on the floor, several feet away, and began paging through newspapers again. They didn’t talk much, Molly seemed genuinely interested in the quest, and in bits and pieces Robert told her about the pharmacy, and why he was suddenly so eager to remember everybody.

Molly was much more efficient at finding the right names and dates than Robert was, and within a few hours, he had scribbled notes for every name on his list. They walked back outside, and Robert stood and waited for his eyes to adjust to the light while Molly locked the library door behind them. There was a disapproving sound from across the street.

“The library’s closed on Sundays,” called someone from an open window in the senior center. Molly rolled her eyes.

“Just ignore them. They always give me grief. They can tell I’m not from here, like you did I guess.”

“It’s your voice,” said Robert. “People around here drop their g’s and grunt their vowels. You talk like somewhere else.”

Molly nodded. “What can I say. This was fun. Do you have more names?”

“There’s a whole graveyard of names. I don’t want to bother you every day though.”

“Oh please, no one comes to the library anymore. This will give me something to do.”

 

So Robert returned to the library the next day, and most of the days after that. They developed a better system, grouping names by time period instead of just where they happened to be buried. They averaged one person every two to three hours, though some were much easier and some took much longer. Where Robert’s notes had focused on who the person was to the town, and how they were known, Molly’s were more imaginative, almost poetic.

“Douglas Goodwin was born on the coldest day of the year and died on the warmest day of the year,” she wrote on one paper, consulting two Almanacs whose bindings had worn off. “Florence Palmer, known for her apple pie, died the winter after the strongest apple harvest of the decade.”

Some deaths were emotionally sad, even a hundred years removed. There was the girl who had been born in a leap year and died at the age of three, before she’d ever had a real birthday. The widow who died of a broken heart two days before her husband returned, unexpectedly alive, from the war (or at least that’s how the headlines told it, a gossip column written a month later hinted that there might have been an affair involved, possibly murder).

There were funny things too, like the rambling old barley farmer who’d bet his friends he could climb the silo without any rope, and lost. They recorded every story on and stored them in the steadily fattening book. They worked well together, picking up where the other left off, swapping papers and continuing the narrative. People who had been forgotten for years suddenly sprang back into existence, estranged from those who had known them as mother or sister, but now connected to two people who knew them only through words and, rarely, photographs.

Arthur glanced over the papers Robert brought home to work on from time to time. Occasionally, he’d recognize a name as a great grandfather of some childhood friend, but mostly he just puttered around the house, enjoying his feeling of liberation from owning the farm. There was a free room in the senior center, which Arthur described as “the single most exciting place he could imagine living.” It made sense. There were people there to cook meals and to clean the bathrooms, and there were people there who liked to spend whole afternoons playing blackjack. Most importantly, there was no manure. On a Wednesday in mid-March, Robert helped his dad move in. As Robert walked down the carpeted hall, carrying a stack of old travel magazines his dad wouldn’t throw out, he heard some of the ladies say to one another, too old to whisper, “that’s the young man who’s always fiddling in the library.” “He’s the one who’s always with that girl.”

“So where are you thinking of living?” asked Arthur as Robert finished straightening the pillows on the bed. “Are you fixing to move in with that library girl? Boy, that’d give the girls down the hall something to talk about.”

“I haven’t really thought about it.”

“Well think it over Bobby. You got a month.”

 

The world of brown and white was beginning to show traces of pink and green. The next day, around noon, Robert was walking through the cemetery with Molly, both Mollies. They had recorded stories for a little more than half the people buried there, nearly two hundred, all of them stored in a thick, leather bound journal. They didn’t know what to do with their list. They thought about publishing some of the more exciting stories in the local paper, or tracking down the descendants of those who’d had families, but choosing people like that seemed unfair. It was enough that the two of them remembered.

Molly, the human, looked down at a simple, granite headstone. Samuel Clarence, 1888.

“These are the saddest stories,” she said. Robert nodded. Samuel was one of the small handful of names they’d been unable to find. They reasoned these people were from another town, or had a nickname, but their empty names haunted Robert.

“We got a lot of them,” Robert said. Molly squeezed his hand.

They wouldn’t have noticed the Escalade slicing down the road, except Molly the dog perked her ears up and growled softly at the sound of the engine. They rushed back to the house, but the two men in suits were already leaving, carrying a signed paper. They simply nodded, got into their car, and drove off.

 Robert pushed inside, not bothering to close the door behind him. It was the sort of spring day when the air tastes sweeter and you don’t mind the chill as much.

“What’s going on, dad?” he asked, seeing Arthur at the table, holding another contract.

“Well, looks like you’ll get to keep working the farm the way you wanted it,” said Arthur. “The ladies down at the old folks’ home will be sad to see me go, but I don’t think we can swing it without selling this place. But maybe with her around it won’t be so bad.” Molly smiled when she pointed at him.

“They’re not buying the house?”

“They say the whole project is off. Apparently Tim’s land was the site of some Civil War training ground or something. One of them preservation societies heard wind of it and got the whole thing shut down. Can you believe it? History was made right there across the road, and here we had no idea of it.”
